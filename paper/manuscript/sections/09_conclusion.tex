\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This study asks a question that precedes router optimization: is the eligible RAG
action pool routable? The best-fixed/oracle decomposition gives a direct answer.
For the 11 resident-eligible actions, both utilities were 12.5\%; all successes came from
one direct route, so strict-success headroom was zero. The learned controller's
validation infeasibility and universal zero-success fallback were therefore not
only a poor policy outcome. They exposed an action set that no selector could use
to improve strict success on the sealed matrix.

The full experiment also identifies a pool with nonzero utility complementarity.
Capable 3B/4B direct and grounded pairs had 13.3--21.7 \pp{} of oracle headroom. With identical
retrieved evidence, their post-retrieval success differed sharply across generators
and tasks. A task-aware choice between direct and grounded operation improved both
strict success and measured generation energy relative to always grounding. The
The observed opportunity lay outside the controller's resident-eligible pool and
was not tested with a prospective selector.

The resulting recommendation is to establish interface validity, capability,
complementarity, cost comparability, and validation selectability before
attributing value to an energy-aware RAG router. Routing algorithms can exploit
differences; they cannot manufacture differences that the action pool does not
contain.
